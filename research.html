Current Research

Adaptive Radiation of Lizards

Several genera have experienced an adaptive radiation, such as textbook favorites like Darwin's Galapagos finches, Hawaiian silverswords, and Carribbean anoles. Arguably, the most extensively studied and well-known adaptive radiation is that of Anolis lizards, but the focus of study is usually on the island radiation and not on the continental counterpart. In fact, many continental adaptive radiations are not as well-studied as their island counterparts because of the complexities inherent in continental systems. Using three lizard families as my focus, I aim to explore the differences between island and continental adaptive radiations, as well as the differences between the radiations of different lizard families.

Family 1: Dactyloidae (genus Anolis). One of the coolest aspects of Anolis adaptive radiation on islands is that the anoles on each island have independently diversified into ecomorphs that allow different species to take advantage of different parts of their environment based on different morphological traits. For the purpose of my research, I am focusing on the well-studied clade Norops. I am working on comparing the diversification rates of the Trachypilus and Placopsis subclades of the Anolis island radiation to their mainland counterpart, the Draconura subclade.

Family 2: Gekkonidae (genus Phelsuma). Phelsuma is a large genus of geckos that originated in Madagascar and radiated out to surrounding archipelagos. Though these geckos have not speciated into ecomorphs like anoles have, they have also taken advantage of the ecological opportunity present on trees.

Family 3: Scincidae (genera Emoia and Ctenotus). The Emoia samoensis species group radiated from the Solomon Islands southeastward to the surrounding Melanesian islands. On nearby Australia, skinks in the Ctenotus genus rapidly diversified in arid regions. These two genera have similar morphological traits that have allowed them to diversify in their respective environments, so they are excellent counterparts for this comparative analysis.

Gopher Tortoise Commensal Diversity

Inspired by the population of relocated gopher tortoises (Gopherus polyphemus) at Circle B Bar Reserve in Lakeland, FL, I worked to determine whether the commensal diversity among this relocated population of tortoises was different from the commensal diversity among an undisturbed, natural population of gopher tortoises located at Lakeland Highland Scrubs.

Mud Turtle Life History and Spatial Ecology

From 2015 to 2017, I worked on a project that aimed to provide a much-needed update to the literature about the life history and spatial ecology traits of striped mud turtles (Kinosternon baurii). Through mark-recapture techniques, we were able to estimate the population size of mud turtles at Circle B Bar Reserve and make general notes about the overall health of the population. We also conducted a telemetry study that provided insight into the homeranges of the turtles.
